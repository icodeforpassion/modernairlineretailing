ATPCO Fare Filing: Fares, Rules & Footnotes — The Deep-Dive Guide

Everything you need to build reliable pricing today and bridge to Offers & Orders tomorrow.

Updated Oct 17, 2025 • ~18 min read • Reviewed for accuracy

Quick jump: Why filed fares still matter • Data lifecycle • Building blocks • Rule categories • Routing & MPM • Surcharges & taxes • Pricing workflow • Monitoring • FAQ

Why filed fares still matter in 2025

Filed fares remain the contractual truth for partners even as airlines race toward dynamic offers. Interline settlement, agency accreditation audits, and clearing house reconciliation all trace back to the filed fare basis and the rule text that authorises its sale. If the filed artefact and the offer you present diverge, somebody later—often a traveler at a check-in desk—pays for the discrepancy.

Teams also underestimate how much policy lives in fare rules. Advance purchase windows, leisure versus corporate eligibility, baggage entitlements, and refund conditions are encoded there. When modernisation programmes accelerate, the safest approach is to bridge from filed semantics to new Offer & Order objects, carrying the rationale with you instead of rewriting policy from scratch.

How fare data actually flows

The lifecycle has more hands in it than most architecture diagrams suggest. Understanding each hop helps you reason about latency, cache drift, and failure modes.

  1. Authoring: Pricing and revenue management teams assemble batches with base fares, rule references, and footnotes. Many airlines still rely on offline tooling, so version control is manual unless you build it.
  2. ATPCO processing: ATPCO validates format, runs reasonability checks, normalises data, and distributes to subscribers ranging from GDS systems to tax engines and third-party data lakes.
  3. Seller ingestion: Aggregators and sellers load the updates into caches. Some apply proprietary optimisation or pruning, which is where inconsistencies often appear.
  4. Shopping and pricing: When a traveler searches, the itinerary, booking codes, and effective dates are matched to the fare records and the compiled rule text to produce priceable candidates.
  5. Ticketing and settlement: Fare basis codes, rule numbers, endorsements, and amounts flow to the ticket or EMD. They drive involuntary servicing, agency debit memos, and interline settlement.
Reality check: Every hop in that chain can drift from host inventory. Automate dataset versioning, surface hashes in your APIs, and reprice at order commit to reduce debit memo exposure.

The building blocks you need to model

Filed data is opinionated but predictable. Capturing these elements cleanly pays dividends when you start layering dynamic offers on top.

  • Fare record: Holds market, directionality, currency, tariff, rule number, effective dates, and the public versus private flag.
  • Fare basis: A shorthand for humans. Always resolve the associated rules to understand what it implies.
  • Rules and categories: Reusable logic blocks that spell out purchase and travel conditions. Treat them as code.
  • Footnotes: Scoped rule fragments that apply only when referenced. Pre-compute merges rather than string-parsing at runtime.
  • RBD mapping: Validates that the inventory bucket (Y, M, J, etc.) is available for each segment.
  • Optional Services and Brands: Carry the content that defines what the traveler actually receives.
  • Construction data (Cat 25/35): Enables transformations and negotiated fares. Handle with the same rigour as public filed content.

Footnotes without the mystery

Footnotes act like mix-ins. When a fare references one, you inherit its rule rows before the pricing engine evaluates categories. A simple optimisation is to generate a deterministic key such as hash(ruleNumber + sortedFootnotes) and cache the compiled structure. That way a given combination is processed once and reused across itineraries.

Routing, MPM, and why both still exist

Routing numbers are prescriptive: they define the exact carriers, via points, and sometimes even equipment you are allowed to use. Maximum Permitted Mileage (MPM) is descriptive: it caps total distance with a percentage tolerance. Many markets publish both. Treat routing checks as grammar validation and MPM as a quantitative guardrail run after fare construction.

Untangling surcharges and taxes

Surcharges and taxes trip up implementations because they originate from different datasets and evaluation points.

  • YQ/YR: Carrier-imposed surcharges filed outside the rule categories. They are market and cabin specific, so keep a matrix keyed by origin, destination, and brand.
  • Category 12: Charges defined inside the rule evaluation. They react to itinerary shape—stopovers, day-of-week, or travel period.
  • Taxes: Always rely on an authoritative tax engine (ATPCO TTBS, ARC, or a certified vendor). Preserve unrounded amounts for audit and apply local rounding rules only at the end.

A pragmatic pricing workflow

The flow below mirrors what we see in production systems that price millions of itineraries per day. It balances speed with auditability.

Input: O&D, dates, passenger mix, cabin, corp codes, itinerary shape

1. Query availability (RBD per segment)
2. Build itinerary candidates (respect connection and elapsed time rules)
3. Fetch fare candidates by market/cabin/effective date/POS
4. Compile base rule + footnotes → structured object with provenance hashes
5. Apply fast filters (Cat 1/2/3/5/15)
6. Validate routing or MPM once the itinerary is anchored
7. Run deep checks (Cat 6/7/8/9/10 + HIP/circle trip)
8. Layer Cat 12 surcharges, then YQ/YR, then authoritative taxes
9. Convert currency, round correctly, emit a full pricing trace

Keeping prices honest

Monitoring filed pricing is a data discipline exercise. A few habits go a long way:

  • Determinism: The same dataset and itinerary should always price the same. Log version hashes alongside the quote.
  • Availability drift: Reprice at order commit and surface quote age so sellers know when to refresh.
  • Regression coverage: Include HIP exceptions, constructed fares, and open returns in automated tests.
  • Tax diffs: Run daily comparisons against the authoritative tax engine to catch configuration mistakes.

Caching that helps instead of hurts

Cache compiled rules and lightweight metadata rather than entire priced itineraries. Invalidate caches based on ATPCO delta messages, and expose a staleness metric so downstream clients can make informed decisions.

Engineering patterns that work

Normalize your internal model

Fare { id, O, D, dir, fareBasis, primaryRBD, tariff, ruleNumber,
       effective, expire, travelStart[], travelEnd[], private, ccy, amount, ptcs[], footnotes[] }

CompiledRule { key, ruleNumber, footnoteSet[], categories{...}, provenance{versions...} }

Availability { segmentKey -> { RBD: seats, updatedAt } }

PricingTrace { itinerarySig, fareComponents[], appliedCats[], surcharges[], taxes[], datasetVersion }

Stage evaluation deliberately

  • Cheap first: Run categories 1/2/3/5/15 before you fan out itineraries.
  • Shape next: Apply routing or MPM once the itinerary is known.
  • Cross-fare checks: Handle combinability, HIP, and circle trip logic once per candidate set.
  • Service later: For Cat 31/33, re-evaluate with the original pricing snapshot during servicing.

Bridging to Offers & Orders

Modernisation does not mean throwing filed data away. Instead, create a unified offer schema that records whether content originated from filed or dynamic logic, store the deltas you apply for continuous pricing, and keep filed attributes around for partners who still rely on them. Interline will lag your in-house capabilities, so transparency matters.

What to monitor

  • ATPCO load freshness, delta lag, and compile backlog metrics.
  • Discrepancy sampling between priced offers and host repricing.
  • Category pass/fail distributions to spot unexpected policy changes.
  • Stage timings and cache hit ratios across the workflow above.
  • Servicing automation success versus manual fallback rates.

Implementation checklist

  • Map lineage from fare → rule → footnote so you can explain every price.
  • Generate itinerary signatures (airports + flights + date + cabin) for audit.
  • Hash compiled rules for reproducibility and troubleshooting.
  • Separate cheap filters from routing/HIP/Cat 10 work to keep response times predictable.
  • Emit a human- and machine-readable pricing trace for servicing teams.
  • Regression-test HIP/circle and constructed fare scenarios before releases.
  • Detect availability drift and stale datasets automatically.
  • Define guardrails for continuous pricing deltas (min/max vs filed ladder).
  • Automate tax-engine configuration updates with diff alerts.
